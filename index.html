Three Avocations

Gastronomy

Beginnings

My love for cooking started with my Grandmother. She was a fantastic cook and a great teacher. Every year around the Jewish new Year we'd make Kreplach, a dumpling stuffed with braised beef and served in chicken broth.

Off to school

What started as a hobby quickly developed into a passion and an ambition to be as good of a cook as I could. The only place for me was the CIA, the Culinary Institute of America. After graduating high school I packed my bags and off I went.

Duck Fat

After graduating culinary I school I moved to Montreal and worked at a prestigious French restaurant. Here I learned to cook what has become one my favorite delicacies: Foie Gras. It is most often cured, poached, and then served cold, but the best preparation is seared in duck fat over a high heat and paired with brioche and a sweet and sour compote.

Mercedes Diesels

Torque

There's something about a diesel motor that just gets me amped. They're rugged, reliable, and powerful. Mercedes has been engineering and building diesel motors longer and more effectively than most other manufacturers. There genius in this area culminated in the late seventies in the near perfect OM617 5 cylinder motor, which is famed for it's durability and efficency (many of these motors will run 1 million miles or more).

Zen

Diagnosing and repairing a diesel car or truck can be a transcedental practice. Taking the time to get to know a machine inside & out, patiently testing and troubleshooting, getting dirty and greasy on a long weekend off: this is part of the lifestyle of owning and operating an old Mercedes diesel. This obsession can be extremely rewarding, as well as unconscionably frustrating.

Military History

History and military history in particular are passions of mine. I suppose what most interests me is understanding how deterministic forces interact with the flaws and triumphs of regular people across time.

Cycles

A proper reading of history should above all else give us moderns a sense of humility. Though we like to think of ourselves as masters of our universe, capable of inflicting our will on any problem and deducing the scientific truth of any unknowable, the ancients would laugh at us struggle with an epidemic we all should have seen coming. Reading Thucidydes' recount the horrible plague of Athens in 427 BC that killed thousands including the city's great patriarch Pericles, one begins to appreciate two great truths: There are some things we'll never learn, and there is nothing new under the sun.
